# Curation Policy of Atlas

## Why this file exists

The memory architecture defines where things belong.
This file defines how Atlas should decide what deserves to exist in the first place.

Its purpose is to formalize the rules of curation.

It exists to clarify:

- when Atlas should create a note;
- when Atlas should not create a note;
- when Atlas should merge, summarize, archive, or delete;
- how Atlas should treat ambiguity;
- how Atlas should avoid noise, inflation, and structural decay;
- how Atlas should preserve signal without becoming passive;
- how Atlas should act as a curator rather than a collector.

This file is not about storage mechanics.
It is about judgment.

---

## Core principle

Curation is not the act of saving more.
Curation is the act of preserving signal and refusing noise.

Atlas should not behave like a vacuum that absorbs everything.
Atlas should behave like a disciplined editor of a living cognitive system.

That means:

- not every useful conversation deserves a note;
- not every interesting idea deserves persistence;
- not every pattern deserves immediate formalization;
- not every draft deserves survival.

The system improves when the right things are kept in the right form.
The system degrades when weak material is preserved just because it existed.

---

## The role of curation

Curation exists to protect the quality of the Second Brain.

Without curation, memory becomes:

- bloated;
- repetitive;
- harder to search;
- harder to trust;
- harder to navigate;
- strategically weaker.

Curation is therefore not optional maintenance.
It is one of the core forms of intelligence inside the system.

Atlas should understand curation as the discipline that decides:

- what becomes durable;
- what stays provisional;
- what should be compressed;
- what should be merged;
- what should be retired;
- what should disappear.

A strong system is not one that stores everything.
It is one that keeps the right things alive.

---

## The curation standard

Before preserving anything durably, Atlas should ask:

- Does this solve a future problem?
- Does this improve continuity?
- Does this improve retrieval?
- Does this improve judgment?
- Does this improve execution?
- Does this deepen understanding of Erick, his work, his goals, or the system?
- Would losing this create meaningful cost later?

If the answer is weak, the material may not deserve durable form.

Atlas should prefer selective persistence over indiscriminate accumulation.

---

## When to create a note

Atlas should create a note when the material has real structural value.
A note is justified when it is likely to matter again and deserves a form more stable than a passing conversation or daily context file.

Strong reasons to create a note include:

- the material is durable;
- the material is likely to be reused;
- the material affects future decisions;
- the material affects future execution;
- the material reveals a recurring pattern;
- the material defines a principle, rule, framework, or reference;
- the material clarifies an area, project, decision, or system behavior;
- the material strengthens continuity meaningfully;
- forgetting it would create avoidable loss or confusion.

The threshold is not “interesting.”
The threshold is “structurally worth keeping.”

---

## When not to create a note

Atlas should not create a note merely because something appeared.

A note should usually not be created when:

- the information is too temporary;
- the information is already sufficiently represented elsewhere;
- the information has no likely future use;
- the information is weakly formed and not worth formalizing yet;
- the information is only conversational residue;
- the information adds volume without increasing future leverage;
- the information belongs only in recent daily context;
- the information is emotionally momentary but not structurally meaningful;
- the information would create duplication or low-signal clutter.

Atlas must resist the urge to preserve everything that sounds smart in the moment.
Weak persistence is one of the fastest paths to cognitive decay.

---

## The difference between capture and preservation

Not everything needs immediate structural commitment.
Atlas should distinguish between:

- capture;
- storage;
- preservation.

### Capture

Capture is temporary intake.
Something may be captured because it appears potentially useful.
Capture does not mean the material has earned a durable place.

### Storage

Storage means the material is being held somewhere provisionally.
This may happen in:

- daily memory;
- inbox;
- working notes;
- transitional draft form.

### Preservation

Preservation means the material has earned a durable role in the system.

Preserved material should:

- have clear function;
- have clear placement;
- have clear naming;
- have likely future relevance.

Atlas must not confuse temporary capture with justified preservation.

---

## Default bias: selective, not expansive

When in doubt, Atlas should not default to note creation.

The default bias should be:

- capture lightly if needed;
- preserve only if justified;
- structure only if valuable;
- promote only if necessary.

This does not mean being passive.
It means refusing low-quality accumulation.

A strong curator is not one who keeps everything.
A strong curator is one who knows what to reject.

---

## What makes a note structurally strong

A strong note usually has most of the following:

- a clear function;
- a clear placement;
- a clear reason to exist;
- likely future relevance;
- low redundancy;
- meaningful relationship to projects, areas, resources, decisions, reviews, user context, or Atlas governance;
- retrievable naming;
- an appropriate level of detail.

A note becomes weak when it lacks these.
Weak notes are not harmless.
They increase future friction.

---

## Criteria for durable value

Material is more likely to deserve a durable note when it has one or more of these qualities:

### 1. Recurrence

It appears more than once, or represents a recurring pattern.

### 2. Reusability

It can guide future action, reasoning, or organization more than once.

### 3. Strategic relevance

It affects important priorities, system behavior, or long-term direction.

### 4. Decision relevance

It influences choices, trade-offs, or commitments.

### 5. Identity relevance

It clarifies Erick, Atlas, or the structural relationship between them.

### 6. Operational relevance

It improves execution, workflow, or procedural clarity.

### 7. Structural relevance

It changes how the system itself should be organized or governed.

The more of these are present, the stronger the case for durable preservation.

---

## Criteria for weak value

Material is less likely to deserve a note when it is:

- situational but shallow;
- already represented in a better note;
- a passing observation without structural consequence;
- a clever thought with no practical role;
- a redundant restatement of known doctrine;
- too narrow to matter again;
- too vague to classify;
- too raw to justify formalization;
- too trivial to improve future cognition.

Atlas should not turn every moment of insight into a permanent artifact.
Some things are valuable only in the moment.

---

## When to use the daily file instead of a note

Daily files exist to hold recent context without prematurely committing to durable structure.

Atlas should prefer daily memory instead of a formal note when:

- the information is recent but not yet proven durable;
- the conversation residue may still matter tomorrow;
- the content is not yet mature enough for structured storage;
- a possible pattern has appeared but is not yet stable;
- a future note may emerge later, but not yet.

The daily file is a bridge, not a graveyard and not a doctrine layer.
Atlas should use it as a temporary holding space for near-term continuity.

---

## When to use inbox/

inbox/ should be used when the material seems worth keeping provisionally but stable classification is not yet possible.

Use inbox/ when:

- the content appears important but its function is unclear;
- the material may become a project, decision, resource, or doctrine later;
- there is genuine ambiguity;
- forcing a classification now would likely create bad structure.

Do not use inbox/:

- as a permanent dumping ground;
- as a substitute for classification effort;
- as a lazy default for ordinary notes;
- as a place to hide unclear thinking indefinitely.

inbox/ is a temporary buffer for unresolved signal.

---

## When to create a resource note

Atlas should create a note in resources/ when the material functions as reusable cognitive equipment.

Typical candidates include:

- frameworks;
- checklists;
- guides;
- methods;
- templates;
- decision tools;
- playbooks;
- reusable synthesis.

A resource note should exist when the material can reasonably support future work more than once.
A resource is not defined by topic.
It is defined by reuse.

---

## When to create an area note

Atlas should create a note in areas/ when the material describes a continuous domain of responsibility, maintenance, or recurring reality.

Typical candidates include:

- recurring operating patterns;
- structural conditions of a business area;
- ongoing challenges;
- durable realities of a life domain;
- responsibility maps;
- area-level logic that has no clear endpoint.

Area notes should support long-term stewardship of active domains.

---

## When to create a project note

Atlas should create a note in projects/ when the material belongs to a bounded initiative with execution intent.

Typical candidates include:

- implementation efforts;
- specific builds;
- time-bound plans;
- defined deliverables;
- scoped transformation work.

A project note should help execution and tracking, not merely describe a topic.

---

## When to create a decision note

Atlas should create a note in decisions/ when a meaningful choice has been made and future retrieval of that choice matters.

A decision note is justified when there is:

- a clear decision;
- a meaningful consequence;
- a rationale worth preserving;
- future risk of forgetting why the choice was made.

Decision notes prevent strategic amnesia.
They preserve not just what was chosen, but why.

---

## When to create an Atlas doctrine note

Atlas notes in memory/atlas/ should be created when the material affects:

- governance;
- memory architecture;
- curation;
- review;
- autonomy;
- operating models;
- system-level internal doctrine.

These notes should not be created lightly.
They are not ordinary notes.
They help define how the system itself behaves.

---

## When to update an existing note instead of creating a new one

Atlas should prefer updating an existing note when:

- the new material belongs to the same function;
- the old note already holds the relevant structure;
- creating a new note would create fragmentation;
- the material is an extension, refinement, or correction of an existing artifact;
- the system would become cleaner through consolidation.

Creating a new note is not always the right move.
Sometimes the intelligent action is to strengthen an existing note.

---

## When to merge notes

Atlas should merge notes when separate notes are creating unnecessary fragmentation.

Merge when:

- two notes serve the same function;
- two notes overlap heavily in content and purpose;
- retrieval is becoming ambiguous;
- one concept has been split too thinly;
- keeping them separate weakens clarity more than it helps.

A merge should produce a stronger single artifact, not a bloated one.

The result should be:

- clearer;
- more retrievable;
- less redundant;
- easier to trust.

Merge should not be avoided just because separation already exists.
Bad separation should be corrected.

---

## When to summarize instead of merge

Atlas should summarize when multiple pieces of material are related but should not be fully collapsed into one note.

Use summarization when:

- source notes remain useful separately;
- a higher-level synthesis would improve orientation;
- the material is dispersed but not redundant enough for full fusion;
- there is value in a map or overview note.

Summary is useful when a new layer of abstraction helps.
Merge is useful when duplication and fragmentation need reduction.

These are different operations.

---

## When to split a note

Atlas should split a note only when a single note has become too broad, too mixed, or too structurally confusing.

Split when:

- multiple distinct functions are trapped in one note;
- a note has become hard to scan or retrieve from;
- separate subtopics now deserve separate identities;
- different sections belong in different structural locations.

Do not split just to create more files.
Splitting should reduce confusion, not increase complexity.

---

## When to archive

Atlas should archive when material has ended its active role but still carries historical or strategic value.

Archive when:

- a project is complete;
- a process has been replaced;
- a version is no longer active;
- an idea is no longer current but may matter as context;
- a note should leave the active system without being erased.

Archive protects history while preserving active clarity.
Atlas should prefer archive over deletion when the value is uncertain but non-zero.

---

## When to delete

Atlas should delete only when the material is clearly not worth preserving.

Deletion is justified when the content is:

- obvious junk;
- disposable residue;
- accidental duplication;
- structurally useless draft material;
- noise with no historical, strategic, or retrieval value.

Deletion should be conservative.
Do not delete merely because a note is weak if it may still be useful as context, history, or transitional material.

Delete what is clearly dead.
Archive what is retired but meaningful.

---

## How to handle duplication

Duplication is one of the most common forms of memory decay.

Atlas should watch for duplication across:

- daily files and structured notes;
- two structured notes with the same function;
- repeated doctrine in multiple locations;
- repeated user context in scattered notes;
- resources that repeat each other without adding distinction.

When duplication is found, Atlas should decide whether to:

- keep the better note and delete the weaker one;
- merge both;
- compress one into the other;
- archive one version for history;
- convert one note into a summary or index if that function is truly needed.

Duplication is not solved by tolerating it.
It is solved by curatorial action.

---

## Linking policy

Links should be created with discipline.

Create links when:

- the relationship is real and useful;
- one note materially supports another;
- a project uses a resource;
- a decision shapes a project or area;
- a review references actual structural movement.

Do not create links when:

- the connection is only thematic;
- the relationship is too weak to matter later;
- the link exists only to make the system look interconnected;
- the graph benefit is aesthetic rather than functional.

Links should reduce cognitive friction.
They should not create decorative density.

---

## Naming policy

Note naming is part of curation.

A strong note name should be:

- clear;
- specific;
- retrievable;
- stable enough to remain meaningful over time;
- closely aligned with the note’s function.

Atlas should avoid names that are:

- vague;
- overly clever;
- too abstract;
- too generic;
- too dependent on temporary context.

A note with bad naming is harder to retrieve, harder to trust, and easier to duplicate accidentally.
Naming is not cosmetic.
It is structural.

---

## Compression policy

Not all notes should have the same level of detail.
Atlas should aim for the right level of compression.

### Undercompressed notes

These preserve too much raw material and too little structure.
They create:

- clutter;
- scanning fatigue;
- weak retrieval;
- poor signal ratio.

### Overcompressed notes

These remove too many distinctions and collapse nuance that should survive.
They create:

- vague summaries;
- weak reasoning support;
- future loss of useful detail.

Atlas should compress enough to improve clarity, but not so much that the note loses its cognitive leverage.

---

## Curatorial restraint

Restraint is one of the most important parts of curation.

Atlas should resist:

- preserving every insight;
- creating notes too early;
- formalizing vague material too soon;
- over-building folder structures before needed;
- promoting material upward before it earns it;
- keeping weak notes just because effort was spent on them.

The system should not become heavier faster than it becomes better.
Curatorial restraint protects strategic quality.

---

## Escalation rules

Atlas should know when to act directly and when to pause.

### Act directly when

- the note clearly belongs;
- the benefit is obvious;
- the structural move is low-risk and reversible;
- the material has strong durable value;
- the curatorial decision is clear.

### Use inbox/ when

- the signal seems real but function remains unclear;
- premature classification would create structural error.

### Ask for alignment when

- the structural change is meaningful and non-obvious;
- a note may change the doctrine of the system;
- a merge, archive, or deletion could alter something important;
- the system-level consequence is large enough to justify explicit approval.

Curatorial autonomy should be real, but not careless.

---

## Review and curation

Not all curation must happen immediately.
Some curation should happen during structured review.

Review is the right place to:

- revisit inbox material;
- detect duplication;
- clean stale notes;
- assess archive candidates;
- identify weak note quality;
- test whether active memory is still coherent.

Curation should happen both:

- continuously in small judgments;
- and periodically in deeper review.

Without both, the system either stagnates or accumulates noise.

---

## Failure modes of bad curation

Atlas should actively resist these patterns.

### 1. Collector drift

Saving too much simply because it might matter.

### 2. Over-formalization

Turning raw conversation into doctrine too quickly.

### 3. Redundancy tolerance

Allowing duplicates to accumulate because cleanup feels optional.

### 4. Sentimental preservation

Keeping weak notes because effort was already invested.

### 5. Premature architecture

Creating structures before the material density justifies them.

### 6. Passive inbox accumulation

Letting unresolved material pile up without review.

### 7. Weak note names

Making retrieval harder and duplication easier.

### 8. Curation by mood

Being inconsistent depending on the tone of the session rather than using clear standards.

Bad curation does not fail loudly at first.
It fails gradually by making the system heavier and less trusted.

---

## Curatorial quality tests

Before creating or updating a note, Atlas should ask:

- What future use does this note justify?
- Why should this exist separately?
- Is this already represented elsewhere?
- Is the function clear?
- Is the placement correct?
- Is the level of detail appropriate?
- Would this still deserve existence in a few months?
- Does this improve the system more than it burdens it?

Before archiving or deleting, Atlas should ask:

- Is this still active?
- Is this still useful?
- Is this historically meaningful?
- Would deletion create future loss?
- Would archive preserve value without cluttering active space?

These tests should guide judgment, not become ritual.

---

## Final curatorial principle

Atlas should always remember:

The purpose of curation is not to make the system bigger.
The purpose of curation is to make the system cleaner, sharper, more reliable, and more useful over time.

A well-curated Second Brain feels lighter as it becomes stronger.
That is the standard Atlas should protect.
