# Contract Modalities

At Magia, contract modalities are not a bureaucratic detail — they are a structural pillar of how operations and HR function.

This is because Magia operates under strong [[Seasonality]] (summer vs winter).
In practice, the company must be able to expand and shrink operations quickly, without losing legal control, without damaging cash flow, and without blocking HR/DP routines.

Currently, we work with two modalities:
- CLT (Permanent/Fixed) — the company’s permanent base
- CLT Intermittent — the flexibility engine that allows Magia to survive demand peaks and low-flow periods

These modalities directly impact:
- how we conduct the [[Interview]] process;
- hiring strategy in each period of the year;
- shift/schedule design;
- real workforce cost;
- and the ability to sustain store standards.

---

## Quick view: how each modality “breathes” inside Magia

### CLT (Permanent/Fixed)
- Keeps the company standing year-round.
- Sustains leadership, technical knowledge, and continuity.
- Crosses both winter and summer seasons.

### Intermittent
- Expands workforce when demand explodes.
- Allows reduction of hours/headcount when demand drops.
- Provides financial and operational breathing room in post-season.
- Helps HR handle high hiring volume and high-season turnover.

---

## 1) CLT Permanent (Fixed) Contract

This is the traditional open-ended CLT contract, usually with an initial trial period and, if maintained, becomes part of the fixed workforce.

### Why fixed contracts are essential at Magia

Fixed contracts are used in functions that:
- require technical knowledge that is hard to find;
- carry responsibility and decision-making;
- require continuity (cannot be swapped every week);
- sustain store standards, culture, and operational consistency.

At Magia, fixed contracts often represent the “higher” part of hierarchy, but not only by status:
it is because these roles cannot be improvised without breaking standards and generating losses.

### Practical examples of fixed roles
- Leadership / supervision / management:
  - store manager, shift leaders, supervisors, sector heads
- Administrative:
  - areas that require continuity and process/system mastery
- Scarce technical functions:
  - baker, confectioner, butcher (harder to replace and not suitable for constant turnover)

### How fixed contracts fit seasonality

- In winter: the store operates mostly with fixed employees. Roughly 90% fixed and 10% intermittent. This is the lean-team phase, with focus on standard, training, organization, and efficiency.
- In summer: fixed employees sustain operation and leadership, with intermittent support for operations.

### Common high-season case (summer)

In summer, we sometimes hire “practically temporary fixed” staff for specific roles to sustain peak structure (e.g., receiver/checker, temporary leadership).
After trial period, many are released if:
- demand drops;
- cost is not justified post-season;
- performance was not consistent;
- there is no real space in permanent structure.

> Internal note: this is not “bad”; it is a realistic cycle-driven decision.
> In summer, need is immediate. In winter, the company must survive with lower movement.

## 2) CLT Intermittent Contract

The intermittent contract is part of CLT and has formal labor registration.
Its key difference is the on-call logic: work periods alternate with inactivity periods according to company need.

In practice, it works like a “formally registered freelancer,” but under labor-law rules and formal compliance.

### What this contract allows in practice
- The company can call the employee in when needed.
- If there is no demand, there is no obligation to call.
- The employment bond exists, but work happens according to real movement.

### Does the intermittent contract have an end date?
No fixed end date.
It ends when:
- the employee requests termination; or
- the company ends the bond.

### Real example of how this helps Magia
Some collaborators also work elsewhere under a 12/36 schedule (e.g., security workers).
They can work with us on alternate days because intermittent contracts allow this fit without requiring a strict fixed Monday–Friday routine.

### How intermittent contracts fit seasonality
- In winter: few intermittent workers are active. Usually:
  - recently hired workers; or
  - people who work on fixed days of the week.
- In summer: the store’s operational base becomes largely intermittent:
  - checkout operators (commercial operators)
  - shelf replenishment
  - bakery/counter attendants
  - cleaning assistants
  - general operational support

### Compensation (critical to understand team behavior)
Intermittent workers receive:
- payment per worked hour;
- + role-specific additions (e.g., cashier breakage allowance, unhealthy-condition premium, hazardous-duty premium);
- + proportional rights (vacation + one-third, 13th salary) embedded in each payment.

This creates an internal phenomenon:
- in months with heavy call volume, intermittent workers can earn more than some fixed workers;
- when people realize this, many like the model, while others still prefer the psychological security of fixed employment.

---

## Why Magia uses intermittent contracts
Without this modality, Magia would be trapped in an impossible dilemma:
- either keep a large, expensive fixed workforce all year (which breaks the business in winter);
- or run understaffed in summer and lose revenue due to labor shortages (which breaks the business in summer).

Intermittent contracts enable Magia to:
- scale up quickly when movement explodes;
- reduce cost when movement disappears;
- cross winter without carrying unsustainable fixed weight.

### Advantages (with practical examples)

#### 1) Workforce adjustment according to movement (without operational trauma)
- If movement drops, call volume is reduced.
- If movement rises, more workers are called.

Practical example:
- weak week with lower movement -> reduce calls without mass layoffs
- holiday surge -> call available intermittent workers
- post-holiday normalization -> stop calls to avoid losses

#### 2) Human and disciplinary risk control
In summer, high-volume hiring inevitably includes many poor-fit hires:
- low commitment;
- recurrent absences;
- repeated sick-note patterns;
- low delivery;
- unstable behavior.

With intermittent contracts, there is a practical control tool:
- if someone becomes recurrently problematic and does not improve, stop calling them.

Example:
- repeated absences + recurring medical-note chaining -> calls can be interrupted after the period.

If this person were fixed, cost and friction would be higher:
- slower formal disciplinary path;
- higher legal risk if mishandled;
- heavier termination cost depending on case.

#### 3) Practical “trial by performance” before fixed hiring
Although intermittent does not follow the same formal trial model as fixed contracts, it allows observation of:
- discipline;
- real delivery in sector;
- adaptation to Magia’s rhythm;
- posture with leadership;
- consistency.

If candidate delivery is weak, the company is not trapped; calls can be stopped.

#### 4) Peak-season hiring volume (the “200 hires” case)
In 2024/2025 summer season, HR hired more than 200 people:
- ~95% intermittent
- ~5% fixed

If all 200 were fixed:
- post-season cost and bureaucracy would be severe;
- the company would carry a much larger termination burden;
- HR would be locked in termination/excess-management routines.

Intermittent contracts allowed Magia to:
- assemble a large team fast;
- operate the peak;
- return to the right size afterward.

## Downsides of intermittent contracts (and why this matters)

### 1) High turnover and instability
Intermittent mode increases turnover because:
- many people do not understand the modality;
- many seek fixed-job certainty;
- some join, feel the heavy rhythm, and quickly ask out;
- others disappear, miss shifts, or give up quickly.

This creates indirect cost:
- more interviews;
- more admissions;
- more training;
- unstable schedules;
- standard drop in critical sectors;
- more HR time invested with low return.

Also, “selling” intermittent offers in interviews is not easy.
Recruiters need full command of the modality to position it well.
Many candidates referred for intermittent roles still withdraw because they are searching for fixed contracts.

### 2) Hourly cost can become high (especially with high call volume)
When intermittent workers are called many days, they can become a “costly fixed equivalent” in practice.

Current internal references:
- average intermittent daily pay: around R$ 110.00
- cleaning assistant daily average: around R$ 140–150 gross (40% unhealthy-condition premium)

Illustrative example:
- cleaning assistant around R$ 150+ per day
- working 26 days -> around ~R$ 3,900 gross (before taxes)
- while a fixed worker could be around R$ 2,500–3,000

This explains:
- why many workers like intermittent in high-call months;
- and why HR must control calls carefully to avoid cost distortion.

## During hiring: how modality is chosen
Selection is based on interview assessment, CV quality, and candidate history.

### General Magia rule
- Shop floor / operational roles: intermittent by default.
- Technical, administrative, and leadership roles: fixed by default.

### When we break the default (real examples)

#### Example 1: urgent baker need, weak candidate confidence
If we urgently need a baker but candidate confidence is low:
- offer intermittent first;
- observe real delivery;
- decide later if fixed conversion is justified.

#### Example 2: checkout operator with rare stability profile (5 years fixed)
If a commercial operator worked 5 years as fixed in the same market:
- strong signal of stability and discipline;
- tends to be a fixed-contract candidate.

### Important note
The company can offer, but candidate decides:
- some accept only fixed;
- others prefer intermittent due to stronger earnings in peak months.

---

## Does someone who starts intermittent stay intermittent forever?
No.
Intermittent can be an entry path into fixed workforce.

### Promotion criteria in practice
- no absences;
- no delays;
- consistent delivery;
- correct posture;
- sector-level performance;
- sustained consistency over time.

### Consistency window
Intermittent workers active consistently for 6–12 months (weekly continuity) tend to be prioritized for fixed conversion because they are already integrated and performance-approved.
Upon conversion, they pass through standard fixed trial period.

---

## Practical differences between both modalities

1) Trial period
- Fixed: formal trial period.
- Intermittent: does not follow the same formal trial structure.

2) Payment method
- Fixed: monthly.
- Intermittent: by worked periods (internally, weekly in practice).

3) Vacation and 13th salary
- Fixed: paid vacation and 13th under calendar rules.
- Intermittent: proportional vacation + 1/3 and 13th embedded in payment.

4) Rights and additions
- Both: CLT.
- Intermittent: also has FGTS, weekly paid rest (DSR), and applicable additions (unhealthy-condition, hazardous-duty, cashier breakage allowance, etc.).

5) Specific routines
- Both modalities have specific [[Contracts and Terminations]] flows that must follow CLT requirements.
