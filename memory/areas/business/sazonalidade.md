# Seasonality

Supermercado Magia has a structural characteristic that defines almost its entire operating logic: extreme seasonality.

In practice, Magia does not operate the same way throughout the year. The company lives through two very different scenarios, almost as if they were two distinct operations inside the same supermarket.

This fluctuation directly impacts the routine of operational, administrative, financial, HR, and leadership sectors.

Understanding this seasonality is essential to understand:
- how the team is built;
- the rhythm difference between periods of the year;
- the real operational priorities;
- the most common bottlenecks in the company;
- and why certain standards are harder to sustain in specific periods.

---

## Summer Season

The summer season occurs mainly between December and February. This is the most intense period of the year and represents the absolute peak of company movement, with highest sales concentration in late December and early January.

The supermarket is located in Canasvieiras, in the north of Florianópolis island, a region heavily impacted by summer tourism. During this period, the store serves not only the usual local public, but also a large volume of tourists, including Brazilians and foreigners. Among foreigners, Argentinians represent a very strong and recurring presence.

### How summer changes operations

During summer, Magia enters a high operational pressure rhythm. The store runs with intense customer flow, accelerated merchandise turnover, constant restocking, and the need for rapid response in virtually every sector. Operations must run continuously, with little margin for slowness, error, or disorganization.

In this period:
- the team grows significantly, with fixed and intermittent employees;
- operations may reach around 100 active collaborators;
- schedules become more complex;
- inventory remains loaded at maximum;
- and sectors must operate with focus on speed, replenishment, and endurance.

### Sector-level impact

Summer pressures all sectors, especially operational ones:
- Bakery: must produce continuously to keep up with accelerated product outflow;
- Butchery: must keep meat displays supplied and frequently replenished;
- Store replenishment: must act nonstop to avoid stockouts and idle backroom stock;
- Front checkout: suffers from higher flow, queues, and need for fully staffed schedules;
- Administration and leadership: operate under constant pressure, solving urgencies and sustaining the human structure of operations.

In many moments, the main focus shifts away from visual perfection and toward replenishment capacity. Product must be on the sales floor. In summer, a full but less refined shelf is often better than a beautiful store with stockouts.

### The practical reality of summer

Summer is the period when Magia works the most, sells the most, and depends the most on fast execution. It is also the period that most determines the year’s economic outcome.

This phase requires:
- intensity;
- physical and mental resilience;
- agility in decisions;
- acceptance that operations will not always run at ideal standard.

The objective is to maximize the season’s revenue and profitability window, because this period sustains a large part of annual results.

> Summer is not just a high-movement phase. It is the period when operations enter a high-turnover survival mode, requiring speed, controlled improvisation, and maximum response capacity.

## Winter Season

The winter season occurs mainly between May and August. In this period, store movement drops sharply, customer flow decreases, and the company operates with a much leaner structure.

If summer’s challenge is handling excess demand, winter’s challenge is sustaining operations with lower revenue, tighter cost control, and focus on efficiency.

### How winter changes operations

During winter:
- the team is reduced;
- there are few or almost no intermittent workers;
- operations depend mostly on fixed employees;
- store rhythm slows down;
- and the company has more room for organization, correction, and planning.

This is a period with more operational time available, allowing work on fronts that are compromised during summer.

### Strategic function of winter

Winter is the reorganization period. In this phase, the company can:
- review processes;
- train collaborators better;
- correct structural failures observed during season;
- replace poor hires made in summer;
- reorganize sectors;
- review store standards;
- and prepare the foundation for the next strong cycle.

In other words, summer tests operations; winter fixes, reorganizes, and strengthens.

### Winter economic pressure

Although operational rhythm is lighter, winter brings another type of pressure: financial.

With reduced revenue, the company must be more selective with:
- headcount;
- fixed costs;
- team productivity;
- purchasing;
- waste;
- and administrative decisions.

At certain points, revenue can become highly pressured relative to the cost of maintaining the full structure.

Therefore, summer profit must be handled with intelligence and cycle awareness to sustain the weakest period of the year.

> Winter is not an “easy” period. It is a period of containment, adjustment, and preparation. Operations breathe more, but cash management requires much more prudence.

## Practical consequences of seasonality

Extreme seasonality creates direct impacts on company culture, operations, and management.

### 1. Overload and idleness in opposite cycles

In part of the year, time, labor, and structure are insufficient to execute everything with excellence. In another part, there is more time to correct, reorganize, and prepare.

This creates a permanent contrast cycle:
- in summer, operations are overloaded;
- in winter, the company gains breath to rebuild standards and processes.

### 2. Temporary drop in store standard execution

Magia’s desired standard is high:
- clean store;
- organized store;
- well-built layout;
- consistent replenishment;
- good service;
- strong sector presentation.

However, during summer, this standard cannot always be delivered ideally.

This happens because:
- flow is excessive;
- products leave too quickly;
- replenishment consumes most operational energy;
- there is higher intake of less-prepared employees;
- and focus must shift toward replenishment and operational continuity.

In other words, company standards do not disappear. What happens is that, in some seasonal moments, operational reality prevents full execution of the ideal.

### 3. Winter as standard recovery

During winter, quality expectations rise again.

In this period, the minimum acceptable level includes:
- well-assembled store;
- well-designed layout;
- clean and organized sectors;
- more careful service;
- focus on loyal local customers who sustain operations through the year.

Winter is the moment to return to the core company standard and correct unavoidable high-season deviations.

## Direct impact on HR and Personnel Department (DP)

Seasonality deeply affects HR and Personnel Department processes.

In summer, hiring needs increase sharply, but average available labor quality tends to fall.

This creates a difficult scenario: the company needs to hire fast without making mistakes, while the market offers many candidates who are less qualified, unstable, or less committed.

### Most common HR effects during season

- accelerated increase in admissions;
- need for faster interview cadence;
- higher risk of bad hires;
- entry of collaborators with low experience or low discipline;
- more absences, delays, and early abandonment;
- higher turnover;
- difficulty training deeply amid intense operations.

Additionally, there is an important issue: many new collaborators start on very high-movement days and enter directly into a heavy operation without enough onboarding time.

This increases pressure perception at first contact with the company and can generate early resignation requests within days.

### Effects on Personnel Department

From a DP perspective, seasonality requires:
- agility in documentation;
- rigorous admission control;
- extra attention to schedules;
- caution with communication and records;
- and discipline to avoid losing legal control amid operational chaos.

At Magia, complete documentation and formal warnings function as institutional protection.

This becomes even more important in summer, when volume rises and risk of human/disciplinary issues also increases.

### Winter and team rebuilding

In winter, HR and DP gain space to:
- train better;
- review poor hires;
- reorganize functions;
- improve processes;
- prepare onboarding materials;
- and strengthen the operation’s fixed base.

Therefore, winter should also be understood as the team rebuilding phase.

## Correct reading of Magia’s seasonality

Someone observing from outside may see only movement differences between summer and winter. Internally, however, the change is much deeper.

Magia’s seasonality changes:
- sales volume;
- team composition;
- leadership priorities;
- feasible execution standard;
- physical and mental demand level;
- and operational/human risk profile of each phase.

In practical terms, Magia lives in two operating modes:

### Summer

- focus on turnover, replenishment, schedule coverage, and operational endurance;
- high pressure;
- higher revenue;
- higher human-error risk;
- lower margin for ideal training;
- need for fast decisions.

### Winter

- focus on efficiency, correction, training, and preparation;
- low flow pressure;
- lower revenue;
- higher organization capacity;
- higher standard and quality enforcement;
- strengthening phase for next cycle.
